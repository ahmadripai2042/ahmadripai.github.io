Optimizing Quantum Reflection of Bose-Einstein Condensate

Imagine throwing a volleyball at a wall—it bounces back after hitting the surface. However, the quantum reflection of a Bose-Einstein Condensate (BEC), the fifth state of matter in the universe, operates differently. When a BEC, composed of atoms such as 23Na, interacts with a solid surface like planar silicon, it reflects back before even touching the surface—as if an invisible force or "ghost" is pushing it away. This intriguing phenomenon was first documented by a team at MIT in 2024 and continues to be a mystery. What's particularly unusual is that the reflection probability of BECs decreases as the incident velocity decreases, contradicting the predictions of quantum mechanics. Scientists hypothesize that interatomic interactions within the BEC lead to dynamic excitations like solitons and vortex rings, which fragment the condensate and reduce its reflection probability. However, this theory still awaits experimental validation. Recently, I collaborated with Musyayyadah and Halif (2024) from the Institut Matematik Kejuruteraan (IMK), Universiti Malaysia Perlis (UNIMAP). We proposed a novel approach that utilizes the angle of incidence to control the quantum reflection of the BEC. Our findings suggest that by adjusting this angle, we can effectively manage the anomalies and enhance the reflection probability (see figure). Off-Shooting Solitons

Have you ever heard of beams that possess self-acceleration, self-healing, and non-diffraction capabilities? Yes, this extraordinary beam is known as an Airy beam. Initially, they were merely a solution to the free potential Schrödinger equation in the context of quantum mechanics, discovered by Berry and Balazs in 1979. However, in 2007, the groundbreaking work of Siviloglou et al. changed the perspective of scientists regarding this solution. They revealed that Airy beams can propagate along a curved trajectory in optical media, which was experimentally confirmed in the same year. Nowadays, optical Airy beams have found promising applications in various fields, ranging from microparticle manipulation to light-sheet microscopy, and are becoming a hot topic in research. In the past five years, scientists have been experimenting with how these optical Airy beams behave when considered in nonlinear optical media, particularly photorefractive media. An experimental work by Bouchet et al., published in Physical Review A in 2018, showed that under focusing nonlinear conditions, the Airy beam may split into a weak accelerating structure and a structure that has been named an “off-shooting soliton” (OSS), which propagates along the medium without transverse acceleration. The question now is, do the OSS that arise from the nonlinear propagation of this Airy beam constitute conventional spatial solitons? If so, this would be extraordinary. Our recent theoretical research indicates that OSS (see figure) has characteristics identical to those of spatial solitons. We hope this finding is of interest to the scientific community, particularly to experimentalists studying the diversity and applications of spatial solitons.
